# Clean Results Section

The current empirical base contains `5` controlled perturbation campaigns and `41` comparison rows. These materials should be read as a working empirical evidence package supporting a follow-up manuscript. The present results describe observed patterns, measured shifts, profile differences, perturbation effects, and stability tendencies within the current local corpus. They do not establish statistical significance, and they should not be generalized beyond the completed campaign set.

Campaign-level summaries show repeated metric movement under controlled edits rather than a single uniform response. Across the current campaign body, campaign means range from cosine signals near `0.973802` to `0.998703`, while mean Euclidean metrics range from `1.601293` to `4.127904`. Within this evidence package, these values are best treated as descriptive indicators of how strongly profile geometry changed under the specific perturbations used in each campaign.

Campaign `empirical-campaign-004` contributes one controlled perturbation series with `11` comparison rows. In that series, light variants such as `edited.txt`, `02_minor_lexical_substitution.txt`, and `09_strong_rewrite_same_claim.txt` showed low perturbation effect signals in the current profile configuration, with Euclidean metrics of `0.321467`, `0.272972`, and `0.225101` respectively. Several structural or compression-related variants showed larger measured shifts, including `01_punctuation_cleanup.txt` at `5.1`, `03_sentence_split_merge.txt` at `5.829847`, `05_compressed_version.txt` at `6.023463`, and `06_expanded_version.txt` at `7.222145`. Other variants, including `04_word_order_shift.txt`, `07_formalized_style.txt`, `08_informalized_style.txt`, and `10_translated_or_crosslingual.txt`, fell into a moderate perturbation range in this profile configuration.

Taken together, these results support a narrow descriptive claim: within the current local evidence package, some controlled edits produce small profile differences while others produce larger measured shifts. The direction and size of the effect depend on the particular variant design, the baseline text, and the metric configuration used in the run bundle. At this stage, the results are most useful as colleague-reviewable empirical materials for a follow-up manuscript rather than as broad claims about text populations.

The current validation layer adds a second descriptive lens over the same empirical base. The released benchmark subset currently contributes `6` baselines and `36` controlled variants, while the validation package now reports repeatable multi-draw random-reference summaries, threshold review across cosine, Euclidean, and Manhattan metrics, and a bridge for the `6` overlapping perturbation axes shared between campaign outputs and benchmark outputs. These additions improve interpretability, but they do not change the bounded status of the current evidence package.

In the current validation package, the pooled random-reference mean Euclidean distance is `5.783678`, with a draw-mean reference interval from `5.536993` to `6.025547`. Against that reference, the empirical campaign mean Euclidean distance of `2.610789` remains lower, which is a useful descriptive contrast for review. It suggests that the observed campaign shifts are not behaving like indiscriminate cross-baseline mismatches in the released benchmark subset. That remains a review-oriented observation rather than an inferential claim.

Threshold review also now spans several metric families. Under the current Euclidean grid, the campaign package yields low=`12`, moderate=`13`, and larger=`16`, while the benchmark subset yields low=`7`, moderate=`13`, and larger=`16`. Under the current cosine grid, the campaign package yields low=`24`, moderate=`5`, and larger=`12`, while the benchmark subset yields low=`23`, moderate=`9`, and larger=`4`. These threshold summaries are useful because they show that interpretation depends partly on which metric family is foregrounded, which is precisely why the current manuscript should keep its results framing descriptive.

The benchmark-versus-campaign bridge is also more explicit in the current package. Among the shared perturbation axes, the closest Euclidean alignment is `word_order_shift` with delta `1.744741`, while the widest current gap is `informalized_style` with delta `7.829415`. This contrast provides a more concrete way to discuss which perturbation families appear to translate more smoothly from the public benchmark subset into the local campaign layer and which still look corpus-bound.

Several limitations should be stated directly alongside the results. The current corpus remains small, the texts are self-authored local research materials, and the completed campaigns do not yet justify population-level generalization. These outputs are not an authorship attribution workflow, not a source-verification workflow, and not a forensic or legal instrument. They are empirical signals attached to provenance-linked local runs, intended to support manuscript preparation and follow-up review.
